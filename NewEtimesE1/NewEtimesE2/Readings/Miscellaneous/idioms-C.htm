Can't Cut The Mustard ( to pass the requirements of membership ... )

To be able to do something at a certain level is to "cut the mustard". If you can't cut the mustard, you aren't allowed to participate.

Example: "Did you hear that Williams got fired?" Answer: "Yes, he couldn't cut the mustard." It is as if cutting the mustard were a certain test that you had to pass in order to belong.

Example: "Bob dropped out of medical school; he couldn't cut the mustard." If you don't work hard enough, or if you just aren't good enough, you can't cut the mustard.

Cold Turkey ( to quit taking a drug by stopping suddenly ... )

To go "cold turkey" is to quit smoking (cigarettes), drinking (alcohol), or taking some other (habit-forming) drug by stopping completely, all at once.

Example: "Did it take you a long time to quit smoking?" Answer: "No, I went cold turkey, and I never went back." People often go cold turkey when they want to stop immediately the problems a drug is creating, but it can be difficult to quit a drug suddenly.

Example: "My doctor gave me morphine to take after surgery, but he gave me less each day so I would not need to go cold turkey." Example: "I want to quit drinking right now. As of this moment, I am going cold turkey."

Come Hell Or High Water ( no matter what else happens ... )

"Come hell or high water" makes clear that some future event will happen and no other event will stop it from happening.

Example: "Will you be at the family reunion next year?" Answer: "Yes- we'll be there, come hell or high water!" Even if events became totally chaotic ("hell") or there were natural disasters ("high waters") the future event would still occur.
*The word "hell" is informal and should not be used in polite situations.

Cross Your Fingers ( let's hope that our wish comes true ... )

An old superstition says that if you "cross your fingers" (usually two fingers of the same hand) it will bring good luck.

Example: "Good luck on your test tomorrow; we are crossing our fingers for you!" Answer: "Thanks!"

Example: "I don't know what Santa Claus will bring for Christmas, but I am crossing my fingers that it will be a bicycle!" The meaning of "cross your fingers" is a bit different when we say "don't" cross your fingers; this means we believe there is a good chance that what we want to happen will not happen. We don't want our expectations to be too high so we say don't cross your fingers; it means don't hope too much for what you want because it probably won't happen.

Example: "It would be great if our team won today, but don't cross your fingers!"

Example: "We hope our son will get accepted to medical school and go on to become a doctor, but we are not crossing our fingers." A common saying: "Let's cross our fingers and hope for the best!"

Cry Over Spilt Milk ( continue complaining about something that cannot be changed ... )

To "cry over spilt milk" is to allow oneself to be bothered by some loss that happened in the past. The analogy is to ones reaction after having spilled (spilt) milk. Once milk has been spilled, it is essentially wasted. The mess can be cleaned up, but the milk is lost. After cleaning up the mess, one must move on. To continue to regret the experience would serve no purpose. When children complain about breaking something which cannot be fixed, their parents often tell them to "stop crying over spilt milk!"

Cry Wolf ( to pretend you need help when you really don't ... )

To "cry wolf" is to complain that you are in danger when you really are not in danger. "Crying wolf" results in other people not believing you when you really do need their help.

Example: "Mom, Dad! Can you please come to my room? I think there is something under the bed!" Answer: "Son, you just go back to bed and stop crying wolf." There is a story about a boy who liked to cry "wolf!" pretending that he was going to be eaten by a wolf; when people came running to help him they found him laughing because he was not really in danger. This happened a number of times and people stopped believing him when he cried "wolf!" Finally, the boy was attacked and killed by a real wolf and no one came to save him because they all thought he was "just crying wolf."

Example: "That kid on the other team just fell down; it looks like he might be hurt!" Answer: "He's not hurt; he's just crying wolf." To "cry wolf" is to call others for help when you do not really need help.

 Curiosity Killed The Cat ( sometimes our curiosity gets us into trouble ... )

"Curiosity killed the cat" recalls a story in which "the cat" was killed because he was too curious and followed "curiosity" too far. "Curiosity killed the cat" reminds us that being too curious can be dangerous.

Example: "That reporter has been asking a lot of questions and the boss doesn't like it." Answer: "Somebody ought to teach him that curiosity killed the cat!" Cats are curious animals who like to investigate things, but their curiosity sometimes takes them places where they might get hurt. Children especially, like cats, are curious and like to test to find out what is dangerous.

Example: "My son just stuck his finger right into the electrical outlet to find out how it would feel. Thank goodness he was okay!" Answer: "Wow, I'm glad he wasn't hurt. Curiosity killed the cat." Example: "Hey, I wonder what's down that street; it looks awfully dark and creepy." Answer: "Let's not try to find out. Curiosity killed the cat."
