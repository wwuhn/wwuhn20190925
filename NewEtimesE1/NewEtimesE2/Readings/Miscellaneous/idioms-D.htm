 Dead Heat ( a winner cannot be determined in this particular contest ... )

This word "heat" is a counting word for a single speed race, such as a swimming or a running race. A dead heat refers to a race that has resulted in a tie. The "heat" is referred to as a "dead" because it is not possible to declare a winner. If the rules of the competition do not allow for a tie, another means must be found to settle the the contest.

 Dog-Eat-Dog ( people are looking out for themselves only ... )

"Dog-eat-dog" describes a world where people will fight for themselves only and are willing to hurt other people. "It's a dog-eat-dog world" suggests that you must first watch out for your own interests because other people will be watching out for theirs.

Example: "I have been in this business for twenty years. It's dog-eat-dog; the competition is always trying to steal your customers." "Dog-eat-dog" says that a person is like a "dog" who will "eat" another "dog."

Note: There is no letter "s" at the end of any of the words in "dog-eat-dog." Example: "That school is dog-eat-dog; students will cheat and even destroy each other's work to get better grades!" Sometimes people say "it's a dog-eat-dog world" to make an excuse when they have done something that hurt another person.

Example: "I can't believe you sold him your motorcycle for so much money!" Answer: "Hey, it's a dog-eat-dog world."

Don't Count Your Chickens Until They're Hatched ( don't make plans based on uncertain future events ... )

"Chickens" start out as eggs, and you can count the eggs, but not all of the eggs will hatch to become chickens. "Don't count your chickens until they're hatched" means don't make plans based on something happening until that thing has already happened.

Example: "Tomorrow I think Bill is going to give you a promotion." Answer: "Well, I won't count my chickens until they are hatched." We cannot know for sure what will happen in the future, so although we can hope for certain things, we can't expect them and we can't make plans based on them happening.

Example: "Our three biggest clients are going to make huge purchases next month; let's celebrate!" Answer: "Let's not count our chickens until they are hatched. I'll wait until next month to celebrate."

Example: "Next Friday I will be able to pay you back that money I owe you." Answer: "I won't be counting my chickens..."

Don't Put All Your Eggs In One Basket ( don't risk everything all at once ... )

To "put all your eggs in one basket" is to risk losing everything all at one time.

Example: "My father is a very careful investor; he never puts all of his eggs in one basket." If you had a certain number of "eggs", it would be safest to put those eggs in different "baskets" and not "put them all in one basket". To "put all your eggs in one basket" would be to risk losing all of your "eggs" in case you drop that one "basket".

Example: "The best way to gamble is to only bet small amounts of money and never put all your eggs in one basket."

Example: "We know that you want to become an actor and we support you, but we still want you to graduate from college. Don't put all your eggs in one basket."

Down To The Wire ( the winner will be determined at the last instant ... )

The "wire" is an analogy for the finish line. The first person to cross the finish line is the winner. When a race is very close it is not clear who the winner is until they "cross the wire." A contest goes "down to the wire" when it comes right down to the finish line before the winner can be determined.

 Drastic Times Call For Drastic Measures ( we need to take extreme actions here ... )

Difficult situations require us to take actions that would be considered extreme if taken in other situations.

Example: "Sales have been slow and we had to let go three of our employees; drastic times call for drastic measures." Actions which are more extreme than we normally take are considered "drastic measures." {also: "desperate times call for desperate measures."}
