Traditional Business Owners Back to Tech School

by Linda Cashdan

　Technology is changing so quickly today that small, traditional business owners need to understand computers, finance, electronic commerce, and upscale marketing techniques just to stay alive. A masters program in technology management at the University of Pennsylvania teaches both technical and managerial expertise.

　Spokesman Lyle Ungar says the program is designed for managers who have been out in the world of business five or 10 years and realize in today's market they must broaden their knowledge. "All of a sudden you want to have this broader understanding," he says. "You need to market the thing you are designing, need to talk to the accountants about getting money for it, need to do an environmental impact study, but you are not trained as an environmental engineer."

　The fact is, Mr. Ungar says, the smallest, simplest businesses have become very complicated. Take grocery stores, for example. Thanks to the advent of the computer, he says, storeowners must gather data in order to sell their merchandise effectively. "They are keeping tremendously detailed track of who is coming in, who is buying what, which products last, how long the shelf life, what should I be selling, where should I be advertising? That is data," he says.

　Student Christian Stein owns six gas stations. He says the knowledge he acquired in a micro-electronics course at the school enabled him to select new gasoline pumps and underground monitoring equipment.

　Inspired by a database management course, he says, he installed web cameras and a computer network. "We have around 50-employees, so there is a lot of room for errors," he says. "So by just putting simple things in place, for example, all our stations have state-of-the-art computer systems in them, we were able to eliminate a massive amount of time that was spent on paper work."

　Trucking company owner Steve Rozman developed a system to track in-transit inventory for his clients after taking the school's electronic commerce course. "The one thing you can be certain about is that there is going to be continual change out there," he says. "So if you are not trying to change and constantly apply technology to business, you are almost guaranteed to lose."

　That philosophy is driving many a business owner back to school these days.
