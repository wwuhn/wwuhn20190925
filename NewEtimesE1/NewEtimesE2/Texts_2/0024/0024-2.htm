Cloze

Japanese Subaru Impreza WRX

by John Birchard

　Japanese automaker Subaru has carved out a nice corner of the U.S. market, establishing itself as the producer of small, moderately-priced all-wheel-drive vehicles with a reputation for reliability. Its newest offering for American buyers might be described using the slang term - hot rod.

　Everywhere but the United States, Subaru is known for its success in world rally competition. And the Subaru that carries the company flag in rallies is the Impreza WRX. Now, Americans have a chance to sample the excitement others have enjoyed.

　At first glance, the Impreza WRX looks like many other modern compact sedans. On closer inspection, though, one sees a large air scoop on the hood, low-mounted fog lamps and wide wheels and tires, clues to the kind of high performance that has made the editor of AutoWeek magazine, Dutch Mandel a fan of the car.

　[Mandel said] "The Subaru WRX has got to be some of the most fun per dollar that anyone in this country can get. You know, for $24,000 or thereabouts, you get 227-horsepower in a two-liter engine that is ... you just, you get a big grin every time you put your foot into it."

　That is the accelerator foot Mr. Mandel is talking about, the foot that activates the turbo-charged power plant and produces plenty of performance. All-wheel drive and a taut suspension, coupled with a five-speed manual transmission, give the feeling of a sports car in this four-passenger, four-door sedan.

　Subaru's Mike Whelan describes the intended customer for the WRX. "Someone who enjoys driving for the pure joy of driving, really. For them, it is not the destination, it is the journey. It is the fun of getting there, shifting through the gears, enjoying the feel of the car, the tightness of the suspension and the chassis, which really makes the driver feel at one with the vehicle," Whelan said. We asked journalist Dutch Mandel if he has any criticisms of the new Impreza. "The only kind of criticism that we have, and I do not know if it is a criticism so much as it is a fact of life, is that it is a hot rod "econobox" (small, inexpensive sedan). But, you know you are not getting a Mercedes. But then, you are not paying Mercedes prices," Mandel said.

　Mike Whelan says Subaru expected buyers of the WRX to be young, but they got a bit of a surprise. "There is quite a wide age range of buyers for the WRX, all the way from about age-25 up to 55 and older," [Whelan said] So the young and the young-at-heart are finding they can obtain fast fun for a relatively low price with Subaru's impressive Impreza WRX. Not a bad recipe for success for any automaker.
